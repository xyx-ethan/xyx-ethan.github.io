    Abstract

    Within-subject motor imagery (MI) EEG classification on Cho2017's six-subject challenge subset poses a selection-overfitting risk: configurations chosen by maximising local cross-validation (CV) accuracy often fail to generalise to the hidden leaderboard. We address this by treating cross-method disagreement as a trial-level calibration signal rather than noise to be averaged away. Our pipeline fuses evidence from five independent method families — a 38-variant EEGNet ensemble, a novel KNN-Retrieval test-time augmentation in the EEGNet penultimate space, 256 CSP bootstrap models, 128 CSSP delay-augmented models, and 32 phase-pair synchrony models — together with an FBCSP V1 anchor. A rule-based selector applies trial-level corrections when evidence from at least four of five families supports them; a Poisson–Binomial tail probability ranks candidate submissions. The final submission (v22) applies exactly four corrections to the strict_replace_B baseline, identifying two spurious EEGNet-driven flips (B-33, B-49) invisible to any single-method analysis. Final leaderboard: 0.920 (+0.010 over FBCSP baseline, +5.4σ over chance).

    1. Introduction

    Brain–computer interfaces (BCIs) based on motor imagery remain a workhorse of assistive technology: a subject imagines limb movement, the imagined movement induces spatially-patterned Mu-wave (8–12 Hz) and Beta-band (13–30 Hz) activity over sensorimotor cortex, and a classifier decodes the laterality. The Codabench BCI--ISLA2026 challenge (challenge id 15044) instantiates this task on six subjects from the Cho2017 corpus: 140 labelled training trials and 60 hidden test trials per subject, 64 channels, 512 Hz, 3-second imagery window.

    Two challenges dominate. First, the data volume per subject is small; three-seed 5-fold CV estimates fluctuate by several points due to fold luck. Second, EEG patterns are highly subject-specific, so global models underperform per-subject tuning. Together these force a design space in which one hyperparameter search per subject can silently select configurations that overfit the CV protocol itself. We quantify this effect: a v2 submission with CV +0.001 over the V1 anchor lost 0.010 on the leaderboard — a 14× overshoot in the wrong direction.

    Thesis. The right response to selection-overfitting is not to pick a better single model but to use disagreement between method families to score individual trial-level decisions. We show this lifts a 0.91 FBCSP baseline to 0.92 with a principled, parsimonious submission.

    2. Method

    2.1 Pipeline Overview

    Figure 1 sketches the architecture. Five independent method families produce per-trial probabilities or vote fractions; an FBCSP V1 anchor supplies the starting submission. A rule-based fusion layer identifies trials where evidence diverges from the anchor; a Poisson–Binomial selector then ranks candidate corrections by the tail probability of exceeding 0.93 on the hidden leaderboard.

    2.2 Deep Family: EEGNet Pretrain–Finetune & KNN-Retrieval

    For each target subject $s \in \{A,\ldots,F\}$, an EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) is pretrained on the 700 training trials of the other five subjects (60 epochs, early-stopping patience 15) and fine-tuned on $s$'s own 140 trials (60 epochs, patience 20) with multi-band augmentation. The augmentation grid sweeps $(F_1,\sigma,t_{\max}) \in \{8,16\}\times\{0.03,0.05,0.07\}\times\{16,32,48\}$ with five seed replicates, yielding 38 distinct variants per subject.

    Our novel contribution here is a KNN-Retrieval test-time augmentation. For each test trial $t$ we extract the penultimate-layer feature $f_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (depthwise-separable conv output, $d = 32 \times T'$). We compute cosine similarity to every training-trial feature $f_i$, take the top $K=10$ neighbours, and define a retrieval probability as:

    $$ P_\text{KNN}(t = c) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{i \in \text{top-}K} w_i\, \mathbb{1}[y_i = c]}{\sum_{i \in \text{top-}K} w_i}, \quad w_i = \exp\!\left(\frac{\cos(f_t, f_i)}{\tau}\right), $$

    with temperature $\tau = 0.3$. The combined prediction is $P_\text{final}(t) = \alpha\, P_\text{EEGNet}(t) + (1-\alpha)\, P_\text{KNN}(t)$, with $\alpha$ tuned on a held-out validation slice.

    Why retrieval works. A standard deep ensemble averages softmaxes of independent models. KNN-Retrieval instead asks a different question: is the softmax supported by the neighbourhood in the model's own feature space? When EEGNet predicts class $c$ with high confidence but its top-10 neighbours largely belong to $\neg c$, the trial is flagged as a suspect — a diagnostic for the deep-only spurious correlation discussed in §4.

    2.3 Classical Families: Bootstrap & Spectral

    An independent classical evaluator produces per-trial flip probabilities from four families. Each family trains $M$ models on bootstrapped subsets of the training trials (80% sample with replacement), and outputs the fraction $p_{\text{family},t} \in [0,1]$ of models that disagree with the V1 anchor for trial $t$.

    • CSP-256. 256 Common Spatial Pattern models, 8 components each.
    • CSSP-128. 128 delay-augmented CSP models (Lemm et al., 2005); spatial filters see one lag copy of each channel.
    • Phase-pair-32. 32 models using inter-channel phase-locking value features.
    • Hybrid-32. 32 models concatenating CSSP and phase features.

    2.4 Rule-Based Trial-Level Fusion

    For each trial, we condense the five-family evidence into a single recommendation. Let $c_\text{V1}$ denote V1 confidence and $p_\text{deep}, p_\text{csp}, p_\text{cssp}, p_\text{phase}, p_\text{hybrid}$ the five family flip probabilities. Two rules cover the high-value cases:

    # (A) Stable removal: V1 confident, ≥4/5 families reject the flip
if c_V1 >= 0.95 and at_least(4, [p_csp <= 0.30, p_cssp <= 0.30,
                                  p_phase <= 0.30, p_hybrid <= 0.30,
                                  p_deep <= 0.30]):
    recommendation = "stable_remove_from_strict"   # revert to V1

# (B) New flip: V1 unsure, ≥3/5 families support the flip
elif c_V1 < 0.70 and at_least(3, [p_csp >= 0.50, p_cssp >= 0.65,
                                    p_phase >= 0.65, p_hybrid >= 0.65,
                                    p_deep >= 0.50]):
    recommendation = "new_flip_candidate_cross_family"   # add new flip

    Rule (A) catches cases where a single-method baseline (strict_replace_B) over-trusted an EEGNet flip that classical methods unanimously reject. Rule (B) catches trials where V1 was unsure and multiple independent families converge on a specific alternative. Our v22 submission applies both rules to produce four trial-level corrections (Table 2 below).

    2.5 Poisson–Binomial Candidate Ranking

    Given multiple candidate submissions differing in the set of corrections they apply, we model the online score as

    $$ \text{score} \;=\; 0.92 \;+\; \frac{n_\text{correct} - n_\text{wrong}}{360}, \qquad n_\text{correct} \sim \text{Poisson-Binomial}(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_K), $$

    where $p_i$ is the success probability of correction $i$ estimated from the fraction of families that agree with it. The Poisson–Binomial PMF is computed exactly by the recursion:

    $$ \pi_k^{(j+1)} \;=\; \pi_k^{(j)}(1 - p_{j+1}) + \pi_{k-1}^{(j)}\, p_{j+1}, $$

    starting from $\pi_0^{(0)} = 1$. From the PMF we compute $P(\text{score} \ge 0.93)$ by summing tail masses and rank candidates accordingly. The v22 submission is selected as the candidate that maximises this tail probability while applying the smallest number of multi-family-supported corrections.

    3. Results

    3.1 Ablation on Subject B

    Subject B is the bottleneck: the V1 anchor's CV is 0.821 — 10 points below the mean. Figure 2 plots the contribution of each pipeline component on 5-seed CV. Cross-subject pretraining contributes the bulk of the gain (+0.015); multi-band augmentation adds a further +0.010; capacity expansion (F1 = 16) and ensembling are essentially flat on CV but improve test-set calibration (see §3.3).

    3.2 Per-Subject Decomposition

    Figure 3 plots V1 anchor CV versus EEGNet ensemble CV across the six subjects. The gap is negative on three subjects (A, C, D — where the V1 FBCSP/CSP baseline already sits above 0.95) and positive only on B, where EEGNet meaningfully improves (0.821 → 0.846). This is the key operational insight: the EEGNet ensemble should be deployed surgically, not globally. v22 keeps V1 for A, C, D, E (with a single E-trial flip), and F; only B receives the full fusion treatment.

    3.3 Trial-Level Audit

    Table 1 documents the four trial-level corrections that v22 applies to the strict_replace_B baseline. Two rows (B-33, B-49) revert strict's flips — they were "deep-only" decisions contradicted by four or more classical families. Two rows (B-37, E-56) add new flips supported by cross-family consensus plus the EEGNet ensemble. Each row's rationale is fully traceable in results/independent_evidence_fusion.csv.

    3.4 Cross-Validation Calibration

    We observe a consistent gap between 3-seed CV (15 folds) and 10-seed bootstrap (50 folds) on Subject B: the same configuration big+aug(σ=0.05, ts=32) produces 0.874 with 3 seeds and 0.846 with 10 seeds — a 0.028 gap that dwarfs any realistic configuration-search signal. We therefore adopt 10-seed bootstrap as the calibrated truth for all upload decisions.

    4. Discussion

    4.1 Deep-Only Spurious Correlation

    The most diagnostically interesting finding is that strict_replace_B, an EEGNet-based submission, confidently flipped two B-subject trials (33 and 49) that four or five classical families unanimously reject. On trial 33, CSP-256 assigns flip probability 0.01, CSSP-128 assigns 0.00, Phase-pair-32 assigns 0.09, and Hybrid-32 assigns 0.06. Yet EEGNet's softmax for "flip" was 0.87. This is not a subtle calibration issue; it is a complete disagreement between deep and spectral representations.

    We conjecture these deep-only flips arise from non-sensorimotor features — EEG artifacts or subject-level signatures that the depthwise-separable convolution latches onto but which do not correspond to the underlying motor-imagery task. Classical CSP/CSSP pipelines, by operating on bandpass-filtered covariance, are insensitive to these non-motor features and correctly abstain. Cross-family evidence fusion therefore acts as a feature-sanity check on the deep model's decisions.

    4.2 When Is This Pipeline Appropriate?

    The methodology is most valuable under three conditions:

    1. Small-data per subject (few hundred trials) where CV fold-luck is substantial.
    2. Availability of orthogonal method families — deep vs. spectral vs. spatial — that capture genuinely different inductive biases.
    3. Hidden-test leaderboards that punish miscalibration.

    For large-data benchmarks (ImageNet-scale), single-family ensembles with clean validation splits remain more efficient. For open-data benchmarks with published test labels, the selection-overfitting risk is absent by construction. But BCI competitions sit squarely in the regime where cross-family calibration pays.

    4.3 Limitations

    The trial-level rules in §2.4 are tuned on our project log, not held-out data. A more principled formulation would learn the family-weighting function from a meta-learning dataset of past BCI competitions. We also rely on the availability of a strong V1 anchor; without FBCSP's 0.91 baseline, the fusion layer would have nothing to correct. Finally, our Poisson–Binomial treatment of per-correction independence is a simplification: corrections on the same subject share variance through the shared training distribution.

    4.4 Negative Results Worth Reporting

    We tested cross-dataset pretraining on Lee2019_MI and PhysionetMI; both were net-negative on Subject B (−0.18 and −0.13 respectively) due to paradigm mismatch (imagined grasping vs. kinaesthetic finger movement; different cue structures). ATCNet underperformed EEGNet at this data scale (−0.15). Mixup (α ∈ {0.1, 0.2, 0.4}), FGSM adversarial training, channel dropout, SWA with various warm-restart schedules, and higher-capacity EEGNets were all flat or worse than the 38-variant aug-ensemble. See results/ablation_study.md for the full list.
