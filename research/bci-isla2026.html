    Per-Subject Adaptive Ensembles for Small-Sample Motor Imagery EEG

    A study note on how to decode imagined hand movement from 64-channel scalp EEG when only 140 labelled trials per subject are available. I compare classical spatial filters, Riemannian-geometric classifiers, and modern deep baselines, and show that the strongest compliant model is not a single architecture but an adaptive per-subject ensemble selected entirely by 5-fold cross-validation. The resulting decoder reaches 0.9512 CV / 0.93 test accuracy on a 6-subject benchmark, matching peer-reviewed SOTA on comparable 2-class motor-imagery tasks. A perhaps-surprising secondary finding: on the strongest subject, a classical CSP + RBF-SVM restricted to the μ-band (8–13 Hz) reaches 0.9786 CV — above a PhysioNet-pretrained EEG-Conformer.

    1. Motor Imagery EEG & the Decoding Task

    A motor-imagery brain–computer interface (BCI) reads sensorimotor cortex activity while a subject imagines a limb movement — without actually moving — and decodes the imagined laterality from scalp EEG. The imagined movement induces a spatially-patterned event-related desynchronisation (ERD) in two frequency bands:

    • μ band (8–13 Hz) — a standing sensorimotor rhythm that is suppressed over the contralateral hemisphere during imagined movement.
    • β band (13–30 Hz) — related contralateral suppression during movement preparation and execution.

    A typical trial: the subject sees a cue ("imagine left hand"), imagines the movement for roughly three seconds, and a classifier maps the 64-channel EEG to left vs. right. The benchmark in this note uses 140 labelled training trials and 60 held-out test trials per subject, six subjects, 64 channels at 512 Hz, with a 2.25 s post-cue window as the default.

    Why small-sample EEG is hard. EEG patterns are highly subject-specific, so a single global model underperforms per-subject tuning. But 140 trials is barely enough for stable 5-fold cross-validation: each held-out fold contains 28 trials, so a single trial flipping changes fold accuracy by 0.036. Re-running the same pipeline with a different CV shuffle routinely produces a 2–3 percentage-point spread — larger than most meaningful configuration differences.

    1.1 Preprocessing

    All models in this note share a common pipeline:

    1. Time crop to drop the cue-reaction onset (~0.75 s) and focus on the stable imagery interval. Two windows are used: the 2.25 s default and a wider 2.5 s variant.
    2. IIR band-pass (MNE-Python's filter_data with method="iir") to the band of interest.
    3. Per-trial z-scoring of each channel, so cross-trial amplitude drift cannot masquerade as a discriminative feature.

    No ICA, artefact rejection, or per-session re-referencing is applied. This keeps the protocol reproducible on any 64-channel EEG dataset without requiring trained analysts.

    2. Why Cross-Validation Variance Is the Core Constraint

    The standard small-sample recipe — grid-search over hundreds of configurations, pick the one with highest 5-fold CV accuracy — quietly violates two assumptions.

    Assumption 1 — CV estimates are low variance. With 140 trials and 5 folds, a single held-out trial swings fold accuracy by 0.036. Re-running the same configuration with different shuffle seeds produces a 0.02–0.03 spread. This is comparable to the size of genuine architectural improvements.

    Assumption 2 — The argmax generalises. Searching over $K$ configurations and picking the top-CV configuration is exactly maximum-of-noisy-sample selection bias. When $K$ is large and the true configuration gap is small, the winner is usually whichever configuration received the most favourable fold assignment — not the configuration that is actually best.

    2.1 Three Design Principles

    Three choices reduce selection-variance harm in the downstream pipeline:

    • Conservative search. Fix hyperparameters that have strong priors (e.g., Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage for covariance estimation); only sweep dimensions the problem is known to be sensitive to (e.g., frequency band, number of spatial components).
    • Per-subject adaptation, not a one-size-fits-all model. Different subjects have different signal-to-noise profiles. Forcing the same model family everywhere hides subject-specific structure.
    • Final-shot submission, no iterative feedback. Once the selection protocol is fixed, averaged per-fold predictions are submitted in a single step. No leaderboard score informs any subsequent model choice.

    3. Candidate Model Families

    Each candidate is a standard published pipeline, evaluated on the same fixed 5-fold StratifiedKFold split so per-fold accuracies are directly comparable.

    Why compare across families rather than within one. Spatial filtering, Riemannian geometry, and deep convolutional networks have qualitatively different inductive biases. When the signal is concentrated in a narrow band and spatially simple (SUB1 in this study), CSP wins; when the covariance matrix is already near-diagonalisable in a broadband (SUB3, SUB4), tangent-space logistic regression wins; when the signal is diffuse and nonlinear (SUB2), a deep convolutional network catches something the classical methods miss. The right question is not "which architecture" but "which architecture for which subject".

    3.1 Common Spatial Patterns → SVM/LDA

    Common Spatial Patterns (Ramoser et al., 2000; Blankertz et al., 2008) solve the generalised eigenvalue problem

    $$ \Sigma_L\, w \;=\; \lambda\,(\Sigma_L + \Sigma_R)\, w, $$

    producing spatial filters whose log-variance on the projected time series discriminates the two classes. Class covariances are regularised with Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage (mne.decoding.CSP(reg="ledoit_wolf")) to stabilise estimation under small-$N$. Downstream classification is either linear discriminant analysis (LDA) or an RBF-kernel support vector machine.

    3.2 pyRiemann Tangent-Space Logistic Regression

    Sample covariance matrices live on the manifold of symmetric positive-definite matrices. Projecting them into the tangent space at their Riemannian mean linearises the geometry (Barachant et al., 2012, 2013), so a standard Euclidean classifier can be applied. In practice: covariance → tangent-space mapping under the affine-invariant metric → L2 logistic regression.

    3.3 EEGNet with Augmentation

    EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) is a compact 1-D separable-convolution network with $\sim$2K parameters. Two layers of learned temporal and spatial filters replace the hand-designed band-pass and CSP respectively; a small separable conv and a linear head close the stack. For the small-$N$ regime we rely on heavy in-batch augmentation (Gaussian noise, time-shift, channel dropout) and a 91-hyperparameter variant pool, evaluated under the same CV split.

    3.4 Stacking Meta-Learner

    Stacking (Wolpert, 1992) fits $K$ base pipelines under cross-validation, collects their out-of-fold positive-class probabilities into a $(n_\text{train}, K)$ meta-feature matrix, and trains a shallow meta-classifier on that. The out-of-fold construction prevents leakage from base-learner training folds into the meta-learner.

    3.5 Test-Time Window Augmentation

    At prediction time, the same base pipeline is trained and evaluated under several overlapping time windows; test probabilities are averaged across windows. This is not test-set feedback: a new classifier is trained for each window using only training data.

    4. A Scientific Finding: μ-Band Spatial Filtering Beats Deep Baselines on the Clearest Subject

    On the subject with the cleanest sensorimotor rhythm (SUB1), the empirically best pipeline is not a deep network, not a broadband Riemannian classifier, but a 4-component CSP on the narrow μ band (8–13 Hz) followed by an RBF-kernel SVM.

    Why this is not obvious. The modern BCI literature increasingly frames deep architectures (EEG-Conformer, LMDA-Net, CTNet, CBraMod) as uniformly dominant. This experiment shows a concrete counter-example: when (a) the signal is narrowly banded, (b) the spatial topography is well-resolved, and (c) the training set is small, a properly-tuned CSP + kernel SVM captures all the discriminative information and any deep model trained on the same 112 trials (five-fold CV leaves 112 in each fold's train split) will tend to overfit to nuisance features. The finding is consistent with the comprehensive benchmark review of Lotte et al. (2018).

    6. Test-Time Window Augmentation

    For SUB3, SUB5, and SUB6 the selected window is the longer 2.5 s variant (samples 256–1537 rather than 384–1537 at 512 Hz). Widening the window back toward the cue onset adds another ∼0.25 s of post-cue data. On this 6-subject benchmark that translated into CV improvements of +0.007 to +0.021 on the three subjects.

    This is a train-time-derived augmentation: each window defines a separately-trained classifier, and only the per-fold test probabilities are averaged. Test-set labels never enter the optimisation loop.

    6.1 Ablation on SUB3

    Window (samples / seconds)5-fold CV

    The extra $\sim$128 samples at the start of the window appear to contain additional sensorimotor transient information the classifier exploits. The effect is not universal: SUB1, SUB2, SUB4 do not benefit from the wider window.

    7. Stacking Meta-Learner for Tangent-Space Variants

    On SUB4, the single best pyRiemann tangent-space pipeline (6–30 Hz) reaches CV 0.9786. A stacking meta-learner combining the out-of-fold predictions of five tangent-space variants (bandpasses 6–30, 8–30, 4–40, 8–13, 6–14) under an L2-regularised logistic regression (C = 10) pushes the CV to 0.9857.

    Let the out-of-fold positive-class probabilities from pipeline $k$ be $\mathbf{p}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n_\text{train}}$. The meta-feature matrix is $P = [\,\mathbf{p}_1\ \cdots\ \mathbf{p}_K\,] \in \mathbb{R}^{n_\text{train} \times K}$. The meta-learner solves

    $$ \widehat{\beta} \;=\; \arg\min_{\beta \in \mathbb{R}^{K+1}}\; \sum_{i=1}^{n_\text{train}} \ell\!\left(y_i,\; \beta_0 + P_{i,\cdot}\,\beta_{1:K}\right) \;+\; \tfrac{1}{2C}\,\lVert \beta_{1:K} \rVert_2^2, $$

    with $\ell$ the logistic loss. Test predictions are produced by feeding each pipeline's averaged test probabilities through the fitted meta-learner.

    Stacking only helps when (a) the base predictions are diverse and (b) no single base model is already near ceiling. On SUB3 the single tangent-space pipeline is already at 0.9929, and stacking leaves it unchanged. On SUB4 the tangent-space family has several near-equally-strong variants that disagree on a small number of trials; the meta-learner exploits exactly that disagreement.

    8. Results & Literature Comparison

    The per-subject adaptive ensemble reaches macro-CV 0.9512 / held-out test accuracy 0.93 (360 trials total, Wilson 95% CI $[0.90,\ 0.96]$). Across recent peer-reviewed 2-class motor-imagery benchmarks:

    What "matches SOTA" means here. 360 trials of held-out test is a small sample: the binomial standard error at 0.93 is ~0.013, so differences smaller than ~0.01 are within test-set noise. Given that, the right reading of the table above is that the adaptive ensemble sits at the top of the published range on the comparable benchmark. Whether it is narrowly above or narrowly below any single peer-reviewed architecture on its native dataset cannot be decided from 60 test trials per subject, and is not claimed.

    8.1 Compliance Note

    Every model-selection decision in this pipeline is made before the held-out test set is consulted. Specifically: (i) candidate pipelines are enumerated in advance, (ii) all candidates share the same fixed StratifiedKFold(random_state=42) split, (iii) the per-subject CV argmax determines the submission, (iv) five per-fold models produce averaged test probabilities, and (v) argmax of those probabilities is submitted — once. No per-submission score ever feeds back into a subsequent model choice.

    9. Takeaways for Computational Neuroscience

    Three transferable lessons.
    1. In small-sample EEG, narrow-band classical methods can beat modern deep architectures. The finding here on SUB1 — μ-band CSP+SVM over PhysioNet-pretrained EEG-Conformer — is not anomalous but expected whenever the discriminative signal is concentrated in a well-resolved frequency band, the topography is clean, and $N$ is in the hundreds rather than the thousands. Deep models need enough training data to recover spatial filters that CSP finds in closed form.
    2. Per-subject heterogeneity is real and should be modelled explicitly. Forcing a single family across subjects sacrifices up to several percentage points on subjects whose signal geometry disagrees with the global choice. The simplest effective response is a per-subject family selection over a small, CV-selected candidate set.
    3. Selection variance is a scientific constraint, not a software bug. With 140 trials per subject, a 5-fold CV swings by 0.03 under different shuffles. Any protocol that uses CV rankings to decide between configurations whose true-CV gap is smaller than this will not generalise. The remedy is conservative search, fixed seeds, and refusing to iterate on held-out scores.
