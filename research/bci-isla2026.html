    Subject-Adaptive Model Selection for Low-Sample Motor-Imagery EEG Decoding

    Rank 1 / 306 at BCI-ISLA 2026 · classical spatial filters, Riemannian geometry, EEGNet, and an off-the-shelf foundation model evaluated under a fixed cross-validation protocol

    A methodological post-mortem of a 6-subject, binary motor-imagery EEG benchmark (140 labelled training / 60 held-out test trials per subject, 64 channels, 512 Hz). I evaluated classical spatial-filtering pipelines, Riemannian classifiers, EEGNet-style deep models, stacking ensembles, and an off-the-shelf EEG foundation model under a fixed cross-validation protocol. The best-performing solution used per-subject adaptive selection and reached 0.9512 selection-CV / 0.93 held-out test accuracy (0.9496 if Subject 3, which sat at the classifier ceiling under a single fixed-seed split, is replaced by a 6-seed stratified-CV robustness mean — see Table 1). The empirical lesson is not that classical methods universally beat deep learning, but that in low-sample subject-specific EEG, narrow-band spatial filters and Riemannian methods remain hard baselines, and off-the-shelf foundation-model transfer requires task- and montage-aware adaptation.

    TL;DR

    1. Finding 1. On the subject with the cleanest sensorimotor μ rhythm, a 4-component CSP + RBF-SVM restricted to 8–13 Hz reaches 5-fold CV 0.9786, compared with 0.7143 for a PhysioNet-pretrained EEG-Conformer and 0.8238 for the best augmented-EEGNet configuration tested — trained and evaluated under identical folds.
    2. Finding 2. No single model family dominates all six subjects. Three model families — narrow-band CSP, pyRiemann tangent-space, and augmented EEGNet — each win on at least one subject, with a pyRiemann-based stacking aggregator additionally selected for Subject 4. Even within CSP, the optimal frequency band and window length vary substantially across subjects (μ 8–13 Hz at 2.25 s for Subject 1; upper-μ 10–14 Hz at 2.5 s for Subjects 5 and 6). Per-subject adaptive selection reaches 0.9512 macro selection-CV / 0.93 held-out test (robustness-adjusted CV, i.e. with Subject 3 reported as a 6-seed mean rather than its fixed-seed 1.000, is 0.9496). This is within the range of strong published two-class motor-imagery decoders, but I do not treat it as a direct SOTA comparison because benchmarks, subject pools, and evaluation protocols differ.
    3. Finding 3. A 5 M-parameter EEG foundation model (CBraMod, ICLR 2025) with its public TUEG-corpus weights, fine-tuned per-subject under the lightweight protocol described below, reaches CV 0.4929 on Subject 1 — indistinguishable from chance. I read this as a protocol-specific negative result rather than a verdict on CBraMod as a model class: it is evidence that clinical-EEG pretraining does not automatically transfer to narrow-band motor imagery without task- and montage-aware adaptation.

    Finding 1 — When classical spatial filters beat deep EEG baselines (Subject 1)

    Subject 1 has a clean sensorimotor μ rhythm: its band-limited trial covariance matrices are well-conditioned and their class means are separable under the affine-invariant Riemannian metric. This is the regime classical spatial filtering is built for. To test how much room modern deep architectures actually have on such a subject, I ran every candidate pipeline under the same fixed 5-fold StratifiedKFold split.

    The ablation over frequency bands under the same CSP+SVM pipeline shows why this is the μ-band specifically:

Adding β-band content does not help on this subject, and under this CSP+SVM protocol the strongest discriminative signal appears concentrated in the μ band.

    The expected effect size of a “strong deep EEG baseline”. Under 5-fold CV on 140 trials, a single held-out fold contains 28 trials — so one more correctly-classified trial is worth 0.0357 of fold accuracy. The 0.26 CV gap between μ-band CSP+SVM and the pretrained transformer is not a rounding artefact; it corresponds to the transformer missing seven extra trials per fold, consistently across folds. The gap is too large to be a finite-sample fluke; it was not closed by the deep-model configurations I evaluated, but I do not claim that all possible deep hyperparameter settings were exhausted.

    Finding 2 — Per-subject heterogeneity forces adaptive model selection

    Running the same pipeline sweep independently for all six subjects reveals sharp subject-level disagreement about which model family is best. No single family dominates all six. Three model families — narrow-band CSP, pyRiemann tangent-space, and augmented EEGNet — each win on at least one subject, with a pyRiemann-based stacking aggregator additionally winning on Subject 4. Even where the same family wins on multiple subjects (CSP+LDA on Subjects 5 and 6), the selected frequency band and window length still differ from the CSP+SVM configuration that wins on Subject 1.

    The obvious shortcut — pick a single globally-best family and use it everywhere — does not work here. Two concrete examples: forcing a narrow-band pyRiemann tangent-space classifier on Subject 1 drops its CV from 0.9786 to 0.9357 (the μ band favours spatial filtering over covariance geometry on this particular subject), and forcing a deep EEGNet-style classifier on Subject 3 drops its CV from near-ceiling to 0.95 (a reproducible deep-vs-classical gap of several points). The per-subject selection step is doing meaningful work beyond what any single family contributes.

    Finding 3 — Off-the-shelf foundation-model transfer was not automatic

    CBraMod (Wang et al., ICLR 2025) is a 5 M-parameter criss-cross transformer pretrained on the Temple University Hospital EEG Corpus (TUEG), the largest publicly-available clinical EEG corpus, with reported evaluation across multiple downstream BCI datasets. As a sanity check I evaluated it on Subject 1, where the classical baselines saturate near ceiling and the μ-band signal is clean — the most favourable subject-specific regime in this corpus.

    Under the lightweight protocol I tested, the off-the-shelf checkpoint did not transfer. Loaded from the official HuggingFace checkpoint (braindecode/cbramod-pretrained) and fine-tuned per-subject under the same 5-fold CV with AdamW(lr=3e-5) for 20 epochs, CBraMod reaches CV 0.4929 on Subject 1 — close to chance given 140 trials. Substituting the same architecture trained from scratch under the same recipe yields CV 0.47, so the pretraining contributed almost no useful prior under this particular fine-tuning recipe. I read this as a protocol-specific negative result rather than a verdict on CBraMod as a model class. A more careful adaptation — channel-aware tokeniser remapping, resampling to the pretraining rate, learning-rate sweep, frozen-encoder linear probe, longer fine-tuning, or task-matched intermediate pretraining — could plausibly close the gap, and was outside the budget of this submission.

    Likely sources of the domain gap (not directly measured here). TUEG-style clinical EEG and cue-locked motor-imagery EEG differ along several axes that have been documented in the broader BCI literature: subject population (clinical vs. healthy), acquisition protocol, montage (variable vs. fixed 64-channel 10–10), sampling rate (typically 250–256 Hz vs. 512 Hz here), artifact profile, and task semantics (passive recording vs. cued sensorimotor imagery). I did not quantify the pretraining-corpus frequency distribution, so I make no specific δ/θ vs μ/β claim. The pretraining objective (masked patch reconstruction) and the downstream task (single-trial 2-class classification) also do not share an intermediate fine-tuning stage that would force representations toward sensorimotor discrimination.

    Methodological framework

    The selection protocol

    For each subject independently:

    1. Enumerate candidate pipelines ahead of time. All candidates share a fixed 5-fold StratifiedKFold(random_state=42) split, so per-fold accuracies are directly comparable.
    2. Pick the argmax-CV pipeline; or, if it is a stacking meta-learner built over several top-CV base pipelines, use its out-of-fold 5-fold CV on the meta-feature matrix as the selection score.
    3. Refit each fold's selected pipeline on its training split, average the five per-fold test probabilities, and argmax to produce the final per-subject label sequence.

    Steps 1–3 never consult the test-set labels or any leaderboard score. Step 3 is executed once, in a single shot.

    Leakage controls

    • All fold-dependent transforms are fit only on the training split of each fold: covariance shrinkage, CSP filters, pyRiemann tangent-space reference means, per-channel scalers, mutual-information feature selectors, and all classifier parameters.
    • Test-time window choice is training-derived. When a 2.5 s window beats the 2.25 s default for a given subject, the choice is made on training-fold CV; a new per-fold classifier is then trained on that window and applied to the matching test window. No leaderboard feedback or test labels are consulted.
    • Leaderboard usage during development: 0 submissions; final evaluation submissions: 1.

    The candidate families

    All candidates are standard published pipelines evaluated on the same fixed folds:

    • CSP + SVM/LDA (Ramoser et al., 2000; Blankertz et al., 2008) with Ledoit–Wolf shrinkage of class covariances. The log-variance of the top k projected components is classified by either an RBF-kernel SVM or linear discriminant analysis.
    • pyRiemann tangent-space + L2 logistic regression (Barachant et al., 2012, 2013). Sample covariances mapped to the tangent space at the Riemannian mean under the affine-invariant metric.
    • EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) with strong in-batch augmentation (Gaussian noise, time shift, channel dropout). A broad hyperparameter sweep over temporal-filter count, augmentation strength, and random-seed realisations is evaluated under the same folds.
    • Filter-Bank CSP (Ang et al., 2012) with nine overlapping bands and mutual-information feature selection.
    • Stacking meta-learner (Wolpert, 1992) over top-CV base pipelines, with an L2-regularised logistic regression on out-of-fold positive-class probabilities.
    • Window augmentation at test time. For three subjects (Subject 3, Subject 5, Subject 6) a wider 2.5 s window beats the 2.25 s default on CV; a new classifier is trained with that window, and only the per-fold test probabilities are averaged — no test-label information ever informs the window choice.

    Test-time aggregation and the stacking protocol

    Final per-subject predictions are produced by CV-bagging: the 5 fold-models of the selected pipeline each predict probabilities on the test set; those 5 test-probability matrices are averaged and argmax'd. Every fold-model is therefore trained on 112 / 140 trials. I deliberately did not perform a post-selection full-140 refit before submission, because (a) the foldwise ensemble provides an internal averaging effect that reduces variance relative to a single full-data fit, and (b) using exactly the foldwise models keeps the test-time protocol bit-identical to the protocol that produced the selection-CV scores. I make no claim that a full-140 refit would have underperformed; it was simply not tested before the single-shot submission.

    For Subject 4's stacking pipeline, the test-time protocol is more involved. Each base tangent-space variant is run through 5-fold CV exactly as above, producing two outputs: an out-of-fold positive-class probability matrix on the 140 training trials (used as meta-feature columns), and an averaged-across-folds test-probability matrix on the 60 test trials. The meta-learner (L2 logistic regression, C=10) is fit once on the (140 × 5) OOF matrix and then applied to the (60 × 5) averaged-test matrix; its 5-fold CV on the OOF matrix is the selection score reported in Table 1. By construction every meta-training row is the prediction of a base model that did not see that row during base training, which is the standard Wolpert leakage-free recipe.

    How to read the numbers

    Held-out test accuracy is the primary external estimate. The 5-fold CV scores are used for subject-level model selection and paired candidate comparison. Because the same finite training set is used to choose among many candidate pipelines per subject, the selected CV is expected to be optimistic and should not be interpreted as an unbiased estimate of generalisation. A nested-CV design would be required for an unbiased training-set-only estimate (Cawley & Talbot, 2010); here, the held-out leaderboard test set provides the external check, and the Rank 1 / 306 figure above is computed on that set.

    The held-out set contains 60 trials per subject × 6 subjects = 360 trials. The leaderboard score is reported as 0.93. If this corresponds to 335/360 correct predictions, the Wilson 95 % confidence interval is approximately [0.900, 0.953]; if it corresponds to 334/360, the interval is approximately [0.896, 0.950]. The exact correct count is the post-challenge label release; the rounded 0.93 is what the leaderboard exposed. Differences of less than roughly 2–3 percentage points between alternative configurations on this set should not be over-interpreted without paired predictions (e.g. McNemar's test or a paired bootstrap).

    Under the same arithmetic, a 3-seed 5-fold CV reshuffle on any single subject produces a 2–3 point spread around its mean. Every candidate in the sweep was therefore evaluated on a single fixed-seed split so that the cross-candidate comparison is on the same partition; this reduces split-induced noise but the selected scores remain subject to finite-sample variance. The Subject 3 row in the table above (fixed-seed 1.000 vs 6-seed mean 0.9905) is a concrete example of how much that variance matters at the ceiling.

    Compliance statement

    Every decision — candidate list, fold split, selection score, window choice, final averaging — is fixed before the test set is consulted. The final submission is produced by a single-shot execution of the protocol; no leaderboard score at any intermediate submission fed back into a later model choice.

    What didn't work (in addition to CBraMod)

    Within the candidate set I evaluated under the fixed protocol, 0.93 test / 0.9512 macro selection-CV was the best compliant solution I found. Every candidate extension I tried failed to improve on it on the training-fold CV.     Two negative results were especially instructive. First, a PhysioNet-pretrained EEG-Conformer on Subject 1 — the easiest subject — lands at 0.71 while a μ-band CSP on the same split reaches 0.98. Cross-dataset pretraining did not close the gap in this low-sample regime, plausibly because the pretraining distribution (a different channel montage, sampling rate, and task mixture) was only tangentially related to the target; I make no claim about the model class itself. Second, confidence-threshold self-training inflated training-fold CV while degrading the calibration of the out-of-fold probability matrix — the classic confirmation-bias signature of pseudo-label methods (Arazo et al., 2020) — and was rejected before any submission was made.

    Limitations

    This is a six-subject, binary motor-imagery EEG benchmark with 140 labelled training trials and 60 held-out test trials per subject. The findings should not be over-extrapolated:

    • The results do not establish that classical methods dominate deep EEG models in general, only that they are hard to beat in this low-sample, subject-specific, narrow-band regime.
    • The CBraMod result is evidence about a particular off-the-shelf checkpoint plus a lightweight fine-tuning recipe, not a verdict on EEG foundation models as a class.
    • The selection-CV score (0.9512, or 0.9496 with the Subject 3 robustness substitution) is used for model selection and is therefore potentially optimistic; the held-out test accuracy (0.93) is the primary external metric.
    • The study does not address cross-session generalisation, online BCI adaptation, clinical deployment, or subject-independent transfer — all of which would change the evaluation protocol substantially.
    • The candidate set, while broad, is not exhaustive. In particular, Riemannian Procrustes alignment, deep tangent-space networks, and sequence-level transformers tuned for sensorimotor rhythms were either tested only at default settings or not tested at all.

    Takeaways

    1. In low-sample EEG regimes with well-resolved rhythms, classical spatial filtering is a hard-to-beat baseline. Not because deep networks are bad, but because a hundred training trials is not enough for them to rediscover the affine structure that CSP recovers in closed form.
    2. Per-subject heterogeneity is the rule, not the exception. A globally-fixed model family is a concession, not a design choice. Adaptive per-subject selection with a small candidate set and fixed CV folds is often the most useful thing a decoder can do.
    3. Off-the-shelf foundation-model transfer is not free. When the pretraining distribution is clinical EEG and the downstream task is narrow-band motor imagery, a 5 M-parameter pretrained encoder can decode at chance under a lightweight fine-tuning recipe. The two approaches are not substitutes without further evidence of representation alignment, but they are also not competitors at this sample size: a serious foundation-model adaptation deserves its own protocol study.

    Reproducibility & references

    The per-subject adaptive ensemble in its clean academic form is packaged as a small Python library at github.com/xyx-ethan/bci-mi-decoder. It wraps MNE-Python, pyRiemann, and scikit-learn, provides a deterministic 5-fold CV harness, and reproduces the table above in a single command. The exact code that produced the submission is permanently tagged at release v0.1.0. The official challenge page and leaderboard are at CodaBench 15044; the Rank 1 / 306 figure above can be verified there.
