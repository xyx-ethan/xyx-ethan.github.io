    Cross-Agent Multi-Family Evidence Fusion for Motor Imagery EEG

    A tutorial on combining independent classifiers for small-data EEG decoding. Covers the motor-imagery classification task, why cross-validation misleads in small-subject regimes, five complementary method families (one deep, four classical spectral/spatial), a novel retrieval-based test-time augmentation, rule-based trial-level fusion, and Poisson–Binomial candidate ranking. The unifying theme: treat disagreement between independent method families as a calibration signal instead of averaging it away.

    1. Motor Imagery EEG & the Classification Task

    A motor-imagery brain–computer interface (BCI) reads sensorimotor cortex activity while a subject imagines a limb movement — without actually moving — and decodes the imagined laterality from scalp EEG. The imagined movement induces spatially-patterned oscillations in two bands:

    • Mu-band (8–12 Hz) — a standing sensorimotor rhythm that is suppressed over the contralateral hemisphere during imagined movement.
    • Beta-band (13–30 Hz) — similar contralateral suppression during movement preparation and execution.

    A typical trial: the subject sees a cue ("imagine left hand"), imagines the movement for three seconds, and a classifier maps the 64-channel EEG to left vs. right. This note focuses on the small-data regime: 140 labelled training trials and 60 hidden test trials per subject, six subjects, 64 channels at 512 Hz, 3-second imagery windows.

    Why small-data EEG is hard. EEG patterns are highly subject-specific, so global models underperform per-subject tuning. But 140 trials is insufficient for fold-stable cross-validation — 3-seed 5-fold CV estimates routinely shift several accuracy points between re-runs. Any configuration chosen by ranking on CV inherits that fold-luck variance.

    1.1 Preprocessing Pipeline

    All methods in this note operate on a shared preprocessing pipeline:

    1. Bandpass the raw signal to 7–30 Hz (captures Mu and Beta; rejects line noise and eye blinks).
    2. Crop the imagery window to drop the cue-reaction lead-in (~0.75 s) and focus on the stable imagery period.
    3. Per-trial z-score so cross-trial amplitude drifts don't mask the spatial pattern.

    2. Why Cross-Validation Misleads in Small-Data Regimes

    The standard recipe for model selection is cross-validation: split training data into folds, train on some, test on others, average. Two assumptions quietly break when the per-subject sample is small.

    Assumption 1 — CV estimates are low-variance. With 140 trials and 5 folds, each held-out fold contains 28 trials; a single flip changes accuracy by 0.036. Running the same configuration with different random seeds produces a 0.03–0.05 spread around the mean — larger than most configuration differences we actually want to detect.

    Assumption 2 — CV ranking generalises. Searching over hundreds of configurations and picking the top-CV configuration is exactly maximum-selection bias: the winner got its gain from fold-luck, not from being better. A +0.001 CV gain can easily lose −0.010 on held-out test data — a 10× overshoot in the wrong direction.

    2.1 Design Response

    Three levers reduce selection overfitting:

    • Higher-seed CV. Moving from 3-seed 5-fold (15 folds) to 10-seed bootstrap (50 folds) shrinks the CV variance until configuration differences become visible against the noise floor.
    • Conservative search. Fix most hyperparameters; sweep only the dimensions you have strong priors about. A full Cartesian grid tends to dredge up overfit winners.
    • Cross-family calibration. The focus of this note: don't rely on a single method's confidence. Combine independent method families and use disagreement as a diagnostic.

    3. Five Method Families: Overview

    If five classifiers with genuinely different inductive biases all agree on a prediction, that prediction is probably right. If they disagree, the prediction is miscalibrated — and the disagreement itself is usable information.

    Independence beats quantity. Five copies of the same model with different seeds form a tight ensemble but share failure modes. Five different model families (spatial filtering, delay-augmented spatial, frequency-domain phase synchrony, hybrid, end-to-end CNN) have different failure modes — so their agreement is much stronger evidence than a same-family ensemble of the same size.

    The five families used in the remainder of this note:

    1. Deep CNN. EEGNet, a compact end-to-end CNN that learns its own spatial and temporal filters.
    2. CSP (Common Spatial Patterns). The classical MI workhorse. Finds spatial filters that maximise the between-class variance ratio.
    3. CSSP (Common Spectral–Spatial Pattern). CSP variant that augments each channel with a delay copy, so the spatial filter can implicitly learn a narrow temporal filter.
    4. Phase-pair. Inter-channel phase-locking values (PLV). Captures cross-channel synchrony rather than amplitude — orthogonal to power-based methods.
    5. Hybrid. Concatenates CSSP and phase-pair features. A belt-and-suspenders family that tends to track whichever underlying signal is cleaner.

    4. Deep Family: EEGNet with Retrieval TTA

    EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018) is a three-layer CNN designed for EEG:

    • Temporal convolution with $F_1$ kernels learns a bank of temporal filters (replacing hand-designed bandpass filters).
    • Depthwise spatial convolution learns per-temporal-filter spatial projections (replacing CSP).
    • Separable temporal convolution then a linear classifier.

    It is end-to-end differentiable, so the usual machinery applies: pretrain, fine-tune, ensemble, augment.

    4.1 Pretrain–Finetune Protocol

    With only 140 trials per subject, training EEGNet from scratch fits noise. The fix is straightforward cross-subject transfer:

    1. Pretrain on pooled trials from all other subjects (700 trials). This teaches EEGNet the general shape of motor-imagery signals.
    2. Fine-tune on the target subject's 140 trials. This adapts to the subject's specific electrode geometry and rhythm.

    Early-stopping with patience 15–20 epochs prevents the fine-tune stage from overfitting.

    4.2 Multi-Band Augmentation Grid

    A second defence against overfitting is data augmentation. The grid sweeps a small set of frequency- and time-domain perturbations:

    • $F_1 \in \{8, 16\}$ — number of temporal filters (model capacity).
    • $\sigma \in \{0.03, 0.05, 0.07\}$ — Gaussian noise injection scale.
    • $t_{\max} \in \{16, 32, 48\}$ — time-shift augmentation range.

    Each cell is trained with five seeds; 38 variants survive calibration on a held-out fold and form the final ensemble.

    4.3 KNN-Retrieval Test-Time Augmentation

    Here is the theoretical contribution. A standard deep ensemble averages softmax outputs. But softmax confidence can lie: a model can be confidently wrong, especially on out-of-distribution trials. Retrieval TTA instead asks: is this prediction supported by similar examples in the model's own feature space?

    Algorithm:

    1. For each test trial $t$, extract the penultimate-layer feature $f_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (the input to EEGNet's final classifier layer; $d = 32 \times T'$).
    2. Compute cosine similarity between $f_t$ and every training-trial feature $f_i$.
    3. Take the top $K = 10$ neighbours. Define the retrieval probability:
    $$ P_\text{KNN}(t = c) \;=\; \frac{\sum_{i \in \text{top-}K} w_i\, \mathbb{1}[y_i = c]}{\sum_{i \in \text{top-}K} w_i}, \qquad w_i = \exp\!\left(\frac{\cos(f_t,\, f_i)}{\tau}\right), $$

    with temperature $\tau = 0.3$. Combine with the EEGNet softmax:

    $$ P_\text{final}(t) \;=\; \alpha\, P_\text{EEGNet}(t) \;+\; (1-\alpha)\, P_\text{KNN}(t), $$

    with $\alpha$ tuned on a held-out validation slice (typically $\alpha \approx 0.6$, meaning the retrieval vote provides a substantial correction).

    Why retrieval TTA works. A trial that sits in a class-$c$ cluster of the feature space has many class-$c$ neighbours — its softmax agrees with its retrieval vote. A trial the model is confidently wrong on sits in the other cluster: its softmax says $c$ but its neighbours say $\neg c$. These are exactly the trials worth flagging. Retrieval TTA is cheap (one cosine-similarity matrix) and catches the failure modes that a plain softmax ensemble cannot.

    5. Classical Families: CSP, CSSP, Phase Synchrony

    Each classical family follows the same recipe: extract features from bandpass-filtered EEG, train a linear classifier, bootstrap the training set to form an ensemble of $M$ models. The bootstrap fraction is 80% of training trials sampled with replacement; each family's output is the fraction of its $M$ bootstrap models that disagree with the anchor's prediction — a number in $[0, 1]$ that we treat as a per-trial flip probability.

    5.1 CSP (Common Spatial Patterns)

    Given bandpass-filtered trial covariance matrices $\Sigma_L$ and $\Sigma_R$ for the two classes, CSP solves the generalised eigenvalue problem

    $$ \Sigma_L \, w \;=\; \lambda\,(\Sigma_L + \Sigma_R)\, w. $$

    The eigenvectors with the largest and smallest eigenvalues form spatial filters that maximise the between-class variance ratio. The log-variance of the top-$k$ filters is the feature vector; an LDA or SVM classifier then decodes left vs. right.

    Ensemble size: 256 bootstrap models, 8 components each.

    5.2 CSSP (Common Sparse Spectral–Spatial Pattern)

    A CSP generalisation (Lemm et al., 2005) that augments each channel $x_c(t)$ with a one-sample delay copy $x_c(t-\tau)$, effectively doubling the channel count. The spatial filter can now implicitly learn a narrow temporal filter — giving CSP a bit of spectral selectivity without an explicit filter bank.

    Ensemble size: 128 bootstrap models.

    5.3 Phase-Pair (Inter-Channel PLV)

    Entirely different representation. For every channel pair $(i, j)$, compute the phase-locking value:

    $$ \mathrm{PLV}_{ij} \;=\; \left|\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} e^{\,\mathrm{i}\,(\phi_i(t)\,-\,\phi_j(t))}\right|, $$

    where $\phi_i(t)$ is the instantaneous phase of channel $i$ obtained from a Hilbert transform of the bandpass-filtered signal. PLV measures how synchronised two channels are in phase, regardless of amplitude. This is orthogonal to CSP and CSSP: a pattern invisible to spatial filtering (because it doesn't change variance) can still be detected by PLV (because it changes synchrony).

    Ensemble size: 32 bootstrap models using PLV features.

    5.4 Hybrid

    Concatenates CSSP and phase-pair feature vectors, trained jointly. Tends to track whichever underlying family has cleaner evidence on a given trial.

    Ensemble size: 32 bootstrap models.

    6. Trial-Level Evidence Fusion

    After the five families score each trial, six numbers per trial are available: the anchor's confidence $c_\text{anchor}$ (a pre-existing strong baseline such as FBCSP), and the five flip probabilities $p_\text{deep}, p_\text{csp}, p_\text{cssp}, p_\text{phase}, p_\text{hybrid}$. The fusion layer condenses these into a decision: should the anchor's prediction for this trial be flipped?

    Two rules cover the high-value cases. A trial must satisfy one rule fully to trigger any modification of the anchor's prediction; otherwise the anchor wins by default.

    # Rule A — stable removal: anchor is confident, ≥4 of 5 families
# reject the flip for this trial. Keep the anchor's prediction.
if c_anchor >= 0.95 and at_least(4, [
        p_csp    <= 0.30,
        p_cssp   <= 0.30,
        p_phase  <= 0.30,
        p_hybrid <= 0.30,
        p_deep   <= 0.30]):
    decision = "keep_anchor"

# Rule B — new flip: anchor is uncertain, ≥3 of 5 families
# converge on a flip. Override the anchor with the family consensus.
elif c_anchor < 0.70 and at_least(3, [
        p_csp    >= 0.50,
        p_cssp   >= 0.65,
        p_phase  >= 0.65,
        p_hybrid >= 0.65,
        p_deep   >= 0.50]):
    decision = "flip_anchor"

else:
    decision = "keep_anchor"
    The asymmetric thresholds (4/5 for Rule A vs. 3/5 for Rule B) reflect the different priors. The anchor is chosen because it's historically reliable, so strong consensus is required to override it. When the anchor is already unsure, lower consensus suffices.

    6.1 What the Rules Catch

    Rule A's archetype is a deep-only spurious correlation: the deep model confidently flips a trial (softmax say 0.87) but all four classical families assign flip probabilities near zero (e.g., 0.01, 0.00, 0.09, 0.06). Four of five families unanimously reject the flip; the rule reverts to the anchor. In practice this corresponds to the deep model latching onto a non-sensorimotor feature — an artefact, eye movement, or subject-identity signature — that the spectral families correctly ignore.

    Rule B's archetype is a cross-family discovery: the anchor was uncertain ($c_\text{anchor} \approx 0.50$), but CSSP, phase-pair, and hybrid all agree on a flip with probabilities above 0.65. Three independent spectral representations converge on the same answer, and the decision reflects that consensus.

    7. Poisson–Binomial Candidate Ranking

    The fusion layer produces a set of candidate corrections. Different subsets of corrections yield different candidate submissions. Which subset should actually be submitted?

    Model the test-set score as the baseline plus a signed sum of correction outcomes:

    $$ \text{score} \;=\; \text{baseline} \;+\; \frac{n_\text{correct} - n_\text{wrong}}{N_\text{test}}, $$

    where each of the $K$ corrections independently succeeds with probability $p_i$ (estimated from the fraction of families supporting that correction). Then $n_\text{correct}$ follows a Poisson–Binomial distribution — the sum of $K$ independent Bernoulli trials with different success probabilities. Its PMF is computed exactly by the recursion:

    $$ \pi_k^{(j+1)} \;=\; \pi_k^{(j)}\,(1 - p_{j+1}) \;+\; \pi_{k-1}^{(j)}\, p_{j+1}, $$

    starting from $\pi_0^{(0)} = 1$ and iterating for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, K - 1$. This yields the probability of exactly $k$ successes; tail probabilities follow by summation.

    Why not plain Binomial? The standard Binomial assumes identical success probabilities, which is unrealistic when corrections have different amounts of cross-family support. Poisson–Binomial relaxes this at the cost of $O(K^2)$ computation instead of $O(K)$ — trivial when $K$ is small.

    7.1 Ranking Candidates

    For each candidate submission, compute $P(\text{score} \ge \text{target})$ by summing tail masses of the Poisson–Binomial PMF. Rank candidates by this tail probability; tie-break in favour of the candidate that applies the smallest set of corrections — an Occam's-razor preference, since fewer moving parts are more trustworthy.

    8. Empirical Results

    Two figures summarise end-to-end behaviour on the six-subject benchmark.

    8.1 Component Ablation

    Starting from the FBCSP anchor on the bottleneck subject (the one where the anchor's CV is weakest), Figure 1 tracks how each component contributes. Cross-subject pretraining dominates (+0.015); multi-band augmentation adds another +0.010; capacity expansion and ensembling are flat on CV but improve calibration under leaderboard conditions; KNN-retrieval TTA and cross-family fusion provide calibration gains that are invisible to plain CV but show up on held-out test data.

    8.2 Per-Subject Decomposition

    Figure 2 plots the anchor's CV against the deep ensemble's CV across the six subjects. The gap is negative on three subjects (where the classical anchor already sits above 0.95) and positive only on the bottleneck subject, where the deep ensemble meaningfully improves (0.821 → 0.846). This is the key operational insight: the deep ensemble should be deployed surgically, not globally. The final pipeline keeps the anchor on subjects where it's already strong and applies the full fusion treatment only to the bottleneck subject.

    8.3 Why Fold Variance Matters

    A consistent gap is observed between 3-seed CV (15 folds) and 10-seed bootstrap (50 folds) on the bottleneck subject: the same configuration produces 0.874 with 3 seeds and 0.846 with 10 seeds — a 0.028 gap that dwarfs most configuration-search signals. This is why configurations selected by plain CV fail to generalise, and why high-seed bootstrap is the calibrated truth to trust for deployment decisions.

    9. Takeaways

    Three portable lessons from this pipeline.
    1. In small-data regimes, treat CV rankings with skepticism. The noise floor of 3-seed 5-fold CV on 140 trials is larger than most configuration differences. Use high-seed bootstrap for calibrated decisions.
    2. Independence is worth more than quantity. Five copies of the same model share failure modes. Five different method families with different inductive biases don't. When they agree, trust them; when they disagree, the disagreement is information.
    3. Deploy deep learning where it helps, not everywhere. A well-tuned classical baseline is often stronger than a deep ensemble on subjects where the signal is already clean. Cross-family evidence fusion identifies the trials where deep learning is actually needed.

    The broader principle: disagreement between independent observers is a calibration signal, not noise. Ensembling by averaging throws this signal away; rule-based fusion over independent families preserves it and turns it into actionable trial-level decisions.
